## Editors Note

As I was reviewing the “Siege Breaker Protocol” and looking ahead to these next chapters on serving others, I stopped cold. I realized I might be giving you whiplash.

In that protocol, we leaned heavily on Ecclesiastes 3:12-13, which tells us that work is a gift! We are supposed to "eat, drink, and find satisfaction in all our toil." That sounds great—work is good, work is satisfying.

But now, we are about to talk about Luke 10:40, where Martha is "distracted by all the preparations" and feeling completely overwhelmed by her service.

So, which is it? Is work a gift that brings satisfaction, or is it a trap that leads to anxiety and burnout? I used to think these were two different conversations. I thought you either "worked hard" or you "rested in spirit." But the secret to spiritual resilience is that how you view your work determines if it burns you out.

Here is the connection that saved me from confusion:

- **Martha’s Mistake:** She wasn't burned out because the work was hard; she was burned out because she was keeping score. She was serving with expectations—expecting help, expecting praise, expecting fairness. When those expectations weren't met, the "toil" became toxic.

- **The Ecclesiastes Gift:** To truly "find satisfaction in your toil," you have to let go of the scorekeeping. You have to serve for the joy of serving, not for the transaction of validation.

As you read these next chapters on serving without expectations, remember this: We aren't asking you to work less or care less. We are asking you to change the audience of your work.

As Colossians 3:23 puts it: _"Whatever you do, work at it with all your heart, as working for the Lord, not for human masters."_

When you serve God (who sees everything) rather than people (who often miss everything), you liberate yourself from resentment. That is the only way to work hard and stay happy.
